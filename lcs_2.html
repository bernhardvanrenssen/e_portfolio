Feedback on Google Translate

Entry 2

Playing around with Google translate has been fun since my first German class in 2007. Countless projects were written in english and translated to German, only to be given bad marks because the teacher could immediately detect the culprits who used this as a cheat. Since 2007 Google translate has become infinately better at translating accurately, and translating entire essays will be well within the range of the capabilitys of today's Google Translate.

As a quick test, I entered a sentence in my home language Afrikaans, and then in Dutch, to see how the automatic detection works:



Google not only easily detected the language, but also managed to pick-up a spelling mistake in my translation. According to the text: "Found in translations: More accurate, fluent sentences in Google Translate", Google uses a Neural Machine Translation system that uses AI to increase the accuracy within its translations. It does this by utilizing an Example Based Machine Translation (EBMT) method where it 'learns from millions of examples' (Schuster, 2016). It translates entire sentinces at a time rather than the primitive 'word-for-word' translation. It does this by encoding the semantics of a sentence rather than using phrase-by-phrase translation (Schuster, 2016).

REFERENCES:

Turovsky, B (2016). "Found in Translation, More accurate, fluent sentences in Google Translate, Google Blog, retrieved August 2021.

Schuster, M. Johnson, M and Thorat,N (2016). Zero-Shot Translation with GOogle's Multilingual Neural Machine Translation System. Google Research Blog, retrieved August 2021
