Seminar 1: Scrum Security Review

The human factor is widely believed to be the wildcard of a security system. Whilst many time and money is spent on mitigations (like user awareness, training and proper eductaion) on this matter, it is proven that despite all these efforts and security procedures, people are the biggest roleplayer in exposing the vulnerabilities in an organization (Dreyer, 2018).

Within the ISO/IEC Standard 27000, there are several terms that can be highlighted to reduce the chance of an internal cyber security breach.

Access control: An integral part of any software development company should be the allocation of access control to all members. By granting every developer administrative access to all files means that sensitive data can easily be leaked and exposed to the end user, leaving it vulnerable for a cyber attack.

Authentication: Typically having user authentication inputs with an identity and password are vulnerable to brute-force strikes, and often clever attackers make use of users' recurring passwords stolen on different sites to gain their sensitive data.Using authentication methods for security-sensitive information within the company can reduce the vulnerability of these components of the system being developed.

Competence: By evaluating your development team members' skills through regular engagement and reviews, employers can determine which developers have the competence to apply certain security measures in a specific project.

Monitoring: Employers or managers of a company have little control over the actions of developers in the company, which often have access to sensitive information that could be used to do harm. Monitoring all employees is a hard task, but risks can be reduced by implementing systems that log actions within a company.

Policy: Protecting sensitive data can be guarded by company policies, and providing upfront training to new developers entering the system to ensure that they are aware of their responsibilities in the fight against cyber attacks.
