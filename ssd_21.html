Debate: Microservices and Microkernels

Monolithic vs Microservice

“Torvalds has been proven wrong and it only took nearly thirty years. Microservices and microkernels are the future.”

Even though the famous ‘Tannebaum-Torvalds’ debate (DiBona & Ockman, 1999) started in 1992 revolved around the opposing kernel architectures namely Microkernel vs Monolithic systems, this debate is certainly as relevant today when observing the architectural differences prevalent in web applications.

Most of the debate Tannebaum and Torvalds (amongst other joining in later…) revolved around defending the system that worked for their specific use-case (Anon, 2016). Although I believe that each architecture has its place (with Linux dominating the smart-device kernel space (Maker, 2009), whereas OS’ on many consumer PCs (Windows) inherently have a monolithic kernel (although it is a bit more complex than this, with drivers being developed separately) (Lynch, 2017).

To expand into microservices vs monolithic web applications, similar to the kernel debate, it would be hard to choose a solid side without considering the application at hand. Where smaller web applications would benefit from the advantages of a monolithic architecture (such as speed of development and cost), larger web applications would certainly be better off when divided into microservices, especially for larger web developer teams working together.

Although I tend to lean towards agreeing with that most modern applications reside on a microservice-type architecture, I also believe that monolithic architectures have their place, and that technology – per definition – will continually change the computing world.
